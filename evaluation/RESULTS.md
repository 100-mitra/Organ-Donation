# Evaluation results — Phase 5 (metrics)

> Generated by `python -m evaluation.run`. **Framing:** these describe **what the mechanism does** — the trade-offs each policy makes — NOT a proof of real-world fairness, which synthetic data cannot establish. The defensible signal is the *relative* shift CAS makes vs the FCFS baseline.

**Config:** 400 recipients, 150 deceased donors, seed 0, longevity weight (on) = 25. Synthetic India ABO/HLA/CPRA distributions (engine/data_gen.py).

### What the numbers say

- **High-CPRA (sensitized) access**: FCFS 35% → CAS 71% — CAS prioritizes hard-to-match patients (cost: low-CPRA 39% → 23%).
- **Pediatric access**: FCFS 38% → CAS 83% → +longevity 100% (cost: adult access falls 37% → 25%).
- **Blood-type-O disadvantage**: under CAS, O 29% vs non-O 42% — it PERSISTS (an ABO-compatibility effect, not fixed by scoring; present under FCFS too: O 36% vs 38%).
- **Waiting time at transplant**: FCFS median 6376d (pure queue) vs CAS 4492d — CAS trades strict seniority for the weighted score.
- **Longevity tension**: turning the longevity weight on drops mean transplant age 30 → 22 — maximizing life-years disadvantages older patients (so it is weight-0 by default).

## Sensitivity — the weights are tunable dials

- **high-CPRA access vs CPRA weight**: 0.31 (w=0) → 0.95 (w=40).
- **pediatric access vs pediatric weight**: 0.29 (w=0) → 1.00 (w=30).
- **median waiting (days) vs waiting-time weight**: 4732.50 (w=0) → 4468.00 (w=60).
- **mean transplant age vs longevity weight**: 29.57 (w=0) → 20.94 (w=40).
